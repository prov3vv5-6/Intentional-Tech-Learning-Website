Making Strong Passwords

Choosing passwords that are easy for you, hard for everyone else.

4 habits
“I use the same password for my email, my bank, and my shopping.”
Reusing one password is the biggest risk of all. If any single site is hacked, thieves try that password everywhere else — and your email and bank fall too. Use a different one for each important account.
“My password is three random words: river-basket-lemon.”
A few random words make a long password that's easy for you to remember and very hard to guess. Length matters far more than adding $ymb0ls to a short word.
“My password is my dog's name, Buddy.”
A pet's name, your birthday, or “password” are far too easy to guess — much of it can be found on social media. Short, personal words are the first thing a thief will try.
“I keep my passwords on a sticky note stuck to my laptop.”
Anyone who sees your screen — a visitor, a repair person — can read it. Writing passwords down is fine, but keep the paper somewhere private and away from the device itself.

Keeping Your Accounts Safe

Simple habits that protect you even if a password slips out.

4 habits
“I let my phone or browser's password manager remember my logins.”
A password manager is like a locked book that fills in your passwords for you. It lets every account have its own strong password without you memorizing any of them. The ones built into your phone and browser are free and safe to use.
“A caller from ‘tech support’ asked for my password to fix my account, so I gave it to them.”
No real company will ever phone and ask for your password — that is always a scam. Never share a password (or a code texted to you) with anyone who contacts you. Hang up.
“I turned on two-step verification — after my password, it texts a code to my phone.”
This is one of the strongest protections there is. Even if someone learns your password, they still can't get in without the code on your phone. Turn it on for your email and bank.
“I write my passwords in a small notebook I keep in a drawer at home.”
This is perfectly fine. A notebook kept private at home can't be hacked over the internet, and it lets you use a different strong password for every account. Far safer than reusing one you can remember.
Remember the big three: make them long, never reuse them, and never share them. 8 habits · 2 groups
